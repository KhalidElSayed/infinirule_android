Help Page for the Infinirule app

Infinirule is a virtual slide rule. It transcends the limitations of real physical slide rules by allowing variable precision—just keep zooming in to get more—and automatically wrapping scales around—like a circular slide rule, but without the bulk. And though it only shows four scales at a time, you can select any combination of the available scale types.

Like most slide rules, Infinirule consists of 3 movable parts: the top 2 scales are part of the body, the bottom 2 are the slide, and there is also the cursor, with a hairline for taking accurate readings. The top two scales move together, as do the bottom two. Dragging the body moves the entire rule left or right, while dragging the slide only moves that. Use two fingers in either the body or the slide to pinch-zoom the rule horizontally to adjust the precision. Dragging in the space below or above the rule scales engages “precision” moving at 1/10th speed, for fine adjustments. You can move either the scales or the cursor in this way.

Long-tap on the scales to bring up a menu that lets you select the scales to show. Long-tap on the cursor to copy/paste scale readings to/from the clipboard.

Note the following points:

  • Any scale may be selected on either the body or the slide. For example, the traditional “K” scale is normally only found on the body, but Infinirule also allows a “y³” scale on the slide. The variable “x” is used for scales on the body, and “y” for ones on the slide.
  • Trig scales are named as arcsine, arccos and arctan, because they are in fact inverse trig functions of the x or y scales.
  • Cosine is separated from sine onto its own scale, rather than having the numbers doubled up on the sine scale as with a traditional slide rule. Similarly, the LLn scales with negative exponents are separated out from the positive ones.
  • The “Wrap” column indicates whether the end of the scale seamlessly continues on from the beginning. Scales are always drawn with adjoining repeats, allowing you to take readings without worrying about running off the end. For ones which don’t meaningfully wrap, a red marker line is drawn on the scale to indicate the discontinuity.
